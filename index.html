Bird Watching Facts

Birdwatching, or birding, is a form of wildlife observation in which the observation of birds is a recreational activity. It can be done with the naked eye, through a visual enhancement device like binoculars and telescopes, by listening for bird sounds, or by watching public webcams. Birdwatching often involves a significant auditory component, as many bird species are more easily detected and identified by ear than by eye. Most birdwatchers pursue this activity for recreational or social reasons, unlike ornithologists, who engage in the study of birds using formal scientific methods.

The early interest in observing birds for their aesthetic rather than utilitarian (mainly food) value is traced to the late 18th century in the works of Gilbert White, Thomas Bewick, George Montagu and John Clare.[7] The study of birds and natural history in general became increasingly prevalent in Britain during the Victorian Era, often associated with collection, eggs and later skins being the artifacts of interest. Wealthy collectors made use of their contacts in the colonies to obtain specimens from around the world. It was only in the late 19th century that the call for bird protection began leading to the rising popularity of observations on living birds. The Audubon Society was started to protect birds from the growing trade in feathers in the United States while the Royal Society for the Protection of Birds began in Britain.

What rare birds you find in a region depends on the season. Click on each season to see what rare birds are spotted during the Summer, Winter, Fall or Spring. Their names and habits are sure to surprise you and make you chuckle too!

The Blue and Yellow Flasher is noted for the dramatic display of its striking undercarriage. It can sometimes be found in "The Rambles" in Central Park during the Summer months.

The lonely male Buzzed Owl seems to have trouble finding a mate. It's numbers are dwindling rapidly. Summers, it can still be found cruising in the North Bronx.

The Meadowlark Lemon is truly a globetrotter. It travels nearly 6,000 miles each year just to spend July and August in the meadows of Central Park.
